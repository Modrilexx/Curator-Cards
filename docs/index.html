Bauhaus (1919–1933)

The Bauhaus school, founded in Germany in 1919 by Walter Gropius, unified art, craft, and industrial production into a single design philosophy. Bauhaus emphasized functionalism, meaning that visual form should follow practical purpose. The curriculum included the Vorkurs foundation course, where students studied geometric abstraction and primary color relationships. Bauhaus designers promoted sans-serif typography and modular composition to achieve clarity and efficiency. The school was closed by the Nazi government in 1933, but its influence spread internationally and shaped modern architecture, graphic design, and industrial design education.

Tags: Vorkurs · geometric abstraction · functionalism · primary colors · sans-serif typography

Examples: MoMA Bauhaus Collection
Bauhaus Archive

Not to be confused with Art Deco because Bauhaus rejected ornament while Art Deco emphasized decorative luxury.

Swiss Style (1950s–1970s)

Swiss Style, also known as the International Typographic Style, developed in Switzerland during the 1950s and emphasized clarity, order, and objective communication. Designers used mathematical grid systems to organize information into clean and readable layouts. Sans-serif typefaces such as Helvetica became central to the movement. Swiss Style favored asymmetrical composition and precise spacing to guide visual hierarchy. The movement influenced corporate identity systems and modern editorial design by establishing standardized approaches to typography and layout.

Tags: grid system · Helvetica · asymmetrical layout · objective design · mathematical grid

Examples: MoMA Typography Collection
Design Museum Swiss Design

Not to be confused with Bauhaus because Swiss Style focused primarily on typography and layout systems rather than total design education.

New Wave Typography (1970s–1980s)

New Wave Typography developed during the 1970s as a reaction against the rigid structure of Swiss Style design. Designers such as Wolfgang Weingart experimented with expressive typography, breaking traditional grid alignment and introducing visual layering. Letters were stretched, rotated, and spaced irregularly to create dynamic compositions. New Wave emphasized personal expression and visual texture rather than strict objectivity. The movement expanded typographic experimentation and influenced digital-era graphic design practices.

Tags: expressive typography · layering · grid disruption · experimental layout · visual texture

Examples: MoMA Graphic Design
Cooper Hewitt Collection

Not to be confused with Swiss Style because New Wave intentionally broke grid rules while Swiss Style depended on strict grid systems.
